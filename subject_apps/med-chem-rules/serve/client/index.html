Extending Lipinski's Rule of Five

Medicinal Chemistry, the development of pharmaceuticals/drugs, is a time, money, and labor-intensive discipline. Many of these resources have to be invested in synthesizing large libraries of compounds in order to find a promising drug candidate. A good drug candidate must have many features, two of which are efficacy and good pharmacokinetics. Achieving both of these in one compound can be difficult because the regime to optimize one often sets back the other.

Medicinal chemists are always looking for general rules to help reduce the number of compounds that need to be made. This means predicting which structures may have favorable properties. Lipinski's Rule of Five, published in 2001 by a scientist at Pfizer, is widely used in Medicinal Chemistry to predict the oral activity of a compound. It is one of the most cited articles in its entire journal. If these guidelines are followed when optimizing the efficacy, it can reduce the likelihood of impairing the pharmacokinetics.

  1. No more than 5 hydrogen bond donors.
  2. No more than 10 hydrogen bond acceptors.
  3. A molecular mass less than 500 daltons.
  4. An octanol-water partition coefficient, log P, not greater than 5.

After 15 years, this rule remains useful and relevant. Can they be iterated upon? The abundance of data, computing power, and practical methods to analyze it have increased dramatically since then.

I would like to use Data Science to see if modern data sets supports or opposes these rules. I would also like to build upon these rules by using machine learning to classify favorable compounds. Introspecting such a model could elucidate more rules for Medicinal Chemists to use in their optimization regimes.
I will get some preliminary data on a list of approved drugs. A list of approved drugs was scraped from this page on the FDA's website. Details on each compound were retrieved from the PubChem API. The data is stored in PostgreSQL and exposed via a Node.js API.
